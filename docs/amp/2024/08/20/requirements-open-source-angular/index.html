Are LLMs going to replace us?

  • Aug 20, 2024
  • post

In this post you can find a short summary of how we make product decisions for Angular. That’s the second time I’m writing this article. The first time I wanted to be as descriptive as possible and the whole thing became as long as a book. Today, I’ll keep it short with the caveat that I’ll probably skip some important details.

Vision

When I think of building a product, I often see it as a sequence of small steps that reach certain global extrema. The global extrema represents the perfect product we want to build. The global extrema is a myth though. You can think about it as a form in Plato’s Theory of Forms - there’s a prefect product that we’re aiming to approximate in the real world. To get there, we can either try to build the perfect thing with a single shot, or approach the problem iteratively. In engineering there’s often this myth of the genius programmer. These 10x coders open their laptop, don’t talk to anyone, they just write code for a couple of weeks, and the result is the perfect product that everyone wants to use. The real world, doesn’t really work this way. Software is complex and is built by teams. Team members need to communicate with each other. In addition to that, we’re building software for our customers. If we don’t talk to them the vision we have will quickly become obsolete. By the time we’re done, the market would have changed and thus this perfect product would look different.

The approach we take with Angular is to focus on a north star - we want to enable developers to build web apps with confidence. The web framework that enables developers to build web apps with confidence also varies over time. In the past 10 years we went though many transformations, which means that while heading to this goal we need to make adjustments. We make these adjustments in a constant feedback loop with developers, other framework authors, and product teams. More about requirements in the next section.

We incorporate this iterative approach in Angular in our planning:

  • We have quarterly objectives in which we reevaluate what we’re working on every three months
  • We have annual strategy to make sure we focus on strategic work
  • We have documents that focus on the long-term vision - next 3-5 years and we use them as our north star

The way we think about our long-term vision document is an abstract representation of the form that we consider to be the perfect framework with our current understanding of the world. We use it as a north star to guide us towards the framework that enables developers to build web apps with confidence. The vision is a living document because things change over time. Assumptions we’re very certain about this quarter can change quickly. Large disruptions, such as GenAI, also require reevaluation of this strategy.

Gathering requirements

People make decisions based on data or feelings. Most frequently it’s a combination of both. When we make decisions based on feelings, ego comes into play as well. There are strong personalities who have an idea what’s good for the product. Literature has shown that’s usually not sustainable 12.

What we care in Angular is to make web developers successful so we listen, prioritize, and execute. It sounds simple, but it quickly gets complicated because of the dozens of sources of requirements. There are millions of developers who provide us feedback constantly in:

  • GitHub issue tracker
  • Chats (Discord) and forums (Reddit, HackerNews, X)
  • Conversations with developers
  • Conversations with GDEs
  • Requests for comments
  • Developer surveys
  • Industry trends
  • Much more!

With this overwhelming amount of requirements sources we try to distill what would be the most impactful for developers. There are lots of challenges here:

  • How do we synthesize the most important information and not get lost in noise?
  • How do we ensure we don’t over index certain developer population?

A challenge that’s often not obvious and hard to solve is ensuring we don’t over index certain developer population. The Angular team members have over 10 years of experience in software development on average, so there’s a risk we target more technical audience with the resources we provide. A different manifestation of this bias is just addressing issues reported on GitHub or X (Twitter). Mostly developers who are passionate about technology use these platforms, which could influence us in building a solution for this developer segment.

To make sure we have a more balanced perspective, we try to build awareness of such biases and balance the developer audiences. For example, explicitly reach out to underrepresented groups and get their feedback. These could be educators, beginners, people from other engineering markets, etc. Other techniques we use are:

  • Developer surveys with tens of thousands of participants. We analyze the open ended answers with data analytics techniques or read them manually if we’re interested in a particular topic
  • Working with agencies for studies that target a particular developer population

Alignment and decision making

We’re consistently working on building alignment. The documents I listed above are there to mostly provide a sense of a common direction the team is heading to. We want to make sure we have a shared vision for Angular and the developers using it.

This doesn’t necessarily mean we should be seeing the exact same picture. That’s unnecessary and counterproductive. We have folks who are experts in server-side rendering (SSR) and others who are proficient in building UI components. They are focused on a particular part of the product. Both groups though, should be aligned on who they are building for and the priorities. For example, if the components team is not aware that SSR is priority, they wouldn’t take hydration into consideration. It sounds trivial, but when a team grows it gets harder to keep this alignment.

That’s the reason we have also folks who operate on a higher-level of abstraction. Instead of being specialist in a particular aspect of the framework, they focus on overseeing the entire product and work on ensuring alignment. This structure doesn’t necessarily imply any direction of the communication - I found it very important for the communication to be bi-directional. Engineers specializing in a particular aspect of the framework provide a crucial perspective that helps shape the product roadmap and people operating on a higher-level of abstraction ensure the team is moving towards a consistent direction.
